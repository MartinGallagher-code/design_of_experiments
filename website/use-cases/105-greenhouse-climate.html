Plackett-Burman Design

Greenhouse Climate Control

Plackett-Burman screening of ventilation rate, shade cloth, CO2 enrichment, heating setpoint, and misting frequency for plant growth and energy cost

Summary

This experiment investigates greenhouse climate control. Plackett-Burman screening of ventilation rate, shade cloth, CO2 enrichment, heating setpoint, and misting frequency for plant growth and energy cost.

The design varies 5 factors: vent rate (ach), ranging from 5 to 30, shade pct (%), ranging from 0 to 60, co2 ppm (ppm), ranging from 400 to 1200, heat setpoint (C), ranging from 15 to 25, and mist freq (per_day), ranging from 0 to 12. The goal is to optimize 2 responses: growth index (pts) (maximize) and energy cost (USD/day) (minimize). Fixed conditions held constant across all runs include greenhouse area = 200m2, crop = cucumber.

A Plackett-Burman screening design was used to efficiently test 5 factors in only 8 runs. This design assumes interactions are negligible and focuses on identifying the most influential main effects.

Key Findings

For growth index, the most influential factors were heat setpoint (44.3%), vent rate (23.5%), mist freq (20.0%). The best observed value was 8.1 (at vent rate = 5, shade pct = 0, co2 ppm = 1200).

For energy cost, the most influential factors were vent rate (37.4%), heat setpoint (36.9%), co2 ppm (11.9%). The best observed value was 6.8 (at vent rate = 5, shade pct = 60, co2 ppm = 400).

Recommended Next Steps

  • Follow up with a response surface design (CCD or Box-Behnken) on the top 3–4 factors to model curvature and find the true optimum.
  • Consider whether any fixed factors should be varied in a future study.
  • The screening results can guide factor reduction — drop factors contributing less than 5% and re-run with a smaller, more focused design.

Real-World Experiment Workflow

ℹ

Physical Agriculture Experiment? Use the Manual Workflow

Agricultural experiments involve real soil, plants, weather, and growing seasons. Results depend on biological processes that can't be scripted. The simulation script above is for demonstration. For your real experiments, use record, status, and export-worksheet.

Manual experiment workflow
# 1. Print a worksheet for the lab / field / kitchen $ python doe.py export-worksheet --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json \ --format csv --output worksheet.csv # 2. Check which runs to do next $ python doe.py status --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json # 3. After each experiment, record the measurements $ python doe.py record --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json --run 1 # 4. Peek at partial results as you go $ python doe.py analyze --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json --partial # 5. Full analysis when all runs are complete $ python doe.py analyze --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json $ python doe.py optimize --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json $ python doe.py report --config use_cases/105_greenhouse_climate/config.json \ --output report.html
✔

Works Without a Test Script

The analysis pipeline doesn't care how results were produced. Record your measurements with record, and the full analysis, optimization, and reporting pipeline works identically. Use --partial to get early insights before all runs are complete.
